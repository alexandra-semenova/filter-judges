New York Immigration Judges' Records May Foreshadow Outcome of Open Asylum Cases

By Alexandra Semenova and Reynaldo Leanos Jr.

Attorney General Jeff Sessions announced in December through a memo that the Department of Justice would hire 60 immigration judges by June 2018 to help reduce the backlog of immigration court cases in the U.S.

The backlog has reached an estimated 657,411 cases, according to data from Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse at Syracuse University. In New York, there are an estimated 89,120 cases in the pipeline.

With days away from the June deadline the Trump administration has only hired five of the 60 proposed immigration judges and no announcement has been made as to where the judges will be placed.

“EOIR constantly monitors its caseload nationwide to determine current and future immigration judge assignments, including shifting resources to meet needs in the most efficient possible manner,” said Kathryn Mattingly, Assistant Press Secretary for the Executive Office for Immigration Review, via email.

Immigration advocates say the placement of judges matter because different regions across the country have various track records for approving, or denying, immigration cases, including those who are seeking asylum.

Cesar Garcia says one reason judges track records for denial and approval rates vary is because some immigration judges come from prosecutorial backgrounds.

“They were quite often prosecutors for the Department of Homeland Security,” said Garcia Hernandez. “When you grow up as a lawyer in a particular institution and your job is to identify who is removable from the United States, and then you get a case with that goal in mind, it’s hard to remove yourself wholly from that perspective.”

There are currently 334 immigration judges in the U.S. and the EOIR says they have streamlined the hiring process for immigration judges from 2 years down to 8 months.
